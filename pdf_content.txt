Date:  April 1, 2026
Version:  1.0
Classification:  Internal — Implementation-Ready Reference
1. Executive Summary
TopRep is a dealership sales performance and analytics platform built on an event-sourced architecture
with Bayesian Monte Carlo forecasting. The system ingests deals and activities via an append-only event
log ( events table), materializes them into queryable tables ( deals, activities ), aggregates monthly
statistics ( rep_month_stats ), and runs 10,000-simulation Monte Carlo forecasts per rep per month.
The platform currently supports: -  6 pipeline stages:  lead → qualified → proposal → negotiation →
closed_won → closed_lost -  11 activity types:  call, email, text, sms, voicemail, meeting, appointment,
test_drive, demo, note, follow_up -  10 activity outcomes:  connected, no_answer, left_vm, appt_set,
showed,  no_show,  sold,  lost,  negotiating,  follow_up  -  3  canonical  lead  sources:  internet,  phone,
showroom
Analytics are computed via a pure-function domain layer with Bayesian shrinkage, anomaly detection,
trust scoring, and scenario analysis.
What exists today:  Event logging, deal/activity materialization, Bayesian forecast engine, rep analytics
with  source  weights  and  expected-unit  calculations,  manager  dashboards  with  coaching  intelligence,
accountability alerts, leaderboard, pipeline Kanban board, and mobile apps.
What  this  document  addresses:  How  to  extend  and  operationalize  the  system  for  full  dealership
workflows — covering deal intake, lifecycle, activity taxonomy, UI presentation, analytics usage, data
modeling, quality control, and design principles.

2.5 Lead vs. Opportunity vs. Deal Distinction
In TopRep's current model, these are stages of the same entity  (the deals table record), not separate
objects:
Term TopRep Status Meaning
Lead status = 'lead' Initial intake, no rep contact yet
Opportunity status ∈ ['qualified', 'proposal',
'negotiation']Rep has engaged; customer has
potential
Deal status ∈ ['closed_won', 'closed_lost'] Resolved outcome
This is the correct design for a dealership tool. Dealership salespeople don't think in "lead vs. opportunity"
CRM jargon — they think in "ups" (new customer encounters) that become "deals" (working a buyer) that
either sell or get lost.
3. Step-by-Step Deal Lifecycle
3.1 Pipeline Stages (Recommended Dealership Model)
TopRep currently uses six pipeline stages. For full dealership coverage, the recommended expanded
pipeline maps to these stages:
Stage Current Status What It Means
New Lead lead Customer record created, no rep action yet
Attempted Contact lead (with activity) Rep tried to reach customer, no conversation
Contacted qualified Two-way conversation achieved
Appointment Set qualified  (with appt) Customer committed to visit
Appointment Shown proposal Customer physically arrived
Working / Needs Analysis proposal Active in-store engagement
Demo / Test Drive proposal  (with test_drive) Customer has driven a vehicle
Negotiation negotiation Numbers presented, structure being worked
Sold closed_won Deal completed

On Hold / Follow-Up Any status with follow_up Customer not ready but has future potential
3.2 Stage Details
New Lead  — Entry events: deal.created  event. Data captured: customer_name, source, raw_source,
deal_amount, arrival timestamp. Key metrics: Speed-to-lead (time to first activity), lead volume by source.
Max duration: 30 minutes  (internet), same-day  (phone/walk-in) — flag if exceeded.

Attempted Contact  — Entry events: activity.completed  with outcome no_answer  or left_vm . Data
captured: Call attempts, voicemails left, texts/emails sent, attempt timestamps. Key metrics: Attempt
count, attempt cadence, channel mix. Max duration: 48 hours  — escalate if no contact after 5 attempts.
Contacted  — Entry events: activity.completed  with outcome connected . Data captured: Connection
timestamp, customer needs identified, vehicle interest. Key metrics: Contact rate, response time, time-to-
contact. Max duration: 3 days  — needs appointment or follow-up plan.
Appointment  Set  —  Entry  events:  activity.completed  with  outcome  appt_set .  Data  captured:
Appointment date/time, confirmed status. Key metrics: Appointment set rate, appointment-to-contact ratio.
Max duration: 5 days  — confirm 24h before, flag unconfirmed.
Appointment Shown  — Entry events:  activity.completed  with outcome  showed. Data captured:
Show timestamp, vehicle preferences, trade-in details. Key metrics: Show rate, no-show rate, time from
set to show. Max duration: Same day  — immediate progression expected.
Working / Needs Analysis  — Entry events: activity.completed  with type meeting . Data captured:
Customer needs, vehicle matches, budget range, credit situation. Key metrics: Time in stage, activity
density. Max duration: 2 days  — if still working without demo, flag.
Demo  /  Test  Drive  —  Entry  events:  activity.completed  with  type  test_drive  or  demo.  Data
captured: Vehicle driven, customer reaction, trade appraisal started. Key metrics: Demo rate, demo-to-
negotiation conversion. Max duration: 2 days  — should lead to write-up.
Negotiation  — Entry events: deal.status_changed  to negotiation . Data captured: Pencil presented,
payment  terms,  trade  value,  financing  options.  Key  metrics:  Negotiation  close  rate,  avg  negotiation
duration, gross per deal. Max duration: 3 days  — flag "stuck negotiations" in accountability.
Sold  — Entry events: deal.status_changed  to closed_won . Data captured: Final deal amount, gross
profit, close date, financing type. Key metrics: Close rate, avg gross, days-to-sale, revenue. Terminal
stage.
Lost  — Entry events: deal.status_changed  to closed_lost . Data captured: Lost reason (price, credit,
not ready, went elsewhere, no vehicle). Key metrics: Lost reason distribution, save attempts, stage-at-loss.
Terminal stage (can reopen).
On Hold / Follow-Up  — Entry events: activity.scheduled  with type follow_up . Data captured: Next
follow-up date, reason for hold, re-engagement plan. Key metrics: Follow-up compliance rate, "be-back"
conversion rate. Max duration: 30 days  — auto-flag stale follow-ups.
3.3 Stage Transition Rules
The current system uses  deal.status_changed  events and  activity.completed  outcomes to track
progression. The funnel module ( lib/domain/funnel/index.ts ) correctly maps activity outcomes to
funnel stages rather than relying solely on pipeline status:
activity.completed + outcome='connected'     → funnel: contact
activity.completed + outcome='appt_set'      → funnel: appointment_set
activity.completed + outcome='showed'        → funnel: appointment_show
deal.status_changed + new_status='closed_won' → funnel: sold
This dual-track system (pipeline status for UI, funnel stages for analytics) is the correct design.

7.2 Schema Design Principles
Activity  types  are  enum-based.  The  CHECK  constraint  on  activities.activity_type  is  correct.
Enum types enable type-safe validation, deterministic funnel mapping, dashboard aggregation without
string matching, and analytics consistency.
Raw source data is preserved separately.  The  raw_source  field on  deal.created  events and the
payload  JSONB column on events table serve as the audit trail. Raw JSONB payload = exactly what
came in, never modified. Normalized fields = what analytics uses.

8.2 Activity Weighting Philosophy
Core principle: Reward outcomes, not efforts.
Tier 1 — Full Weight (1.0):  Connected call, appointment set, appointment showed, test drive, deal closed
Tier 2 — Partial Weight (0.3–0.5):  Outbound call no answer, voicemail left, email sent, text sent with no
response
Tier 3 — Zero Weight (performance):  Notes added, follow-up scheduled, automated emails, internal
messages

9. Final Recommended Design Principles
Principle 1: Events Are the Source of Truth
Everything flows from the append-only event log. Never modify events. Always recompute derived state
from events when in doubt ( replayEventsToStats() ). Preserve raw payloads — they're your audit trail.
Principle 2: Funnel Stages Come from Activity Outcomes, Not Deal Status
The deal status is a UI/workflow construct. The analytical funnel comes from activity outcomes. A deal can
be in "proposal" status without the customer being contacted. The Bayesian engine needs stage-by-stage
conversion rates, not status transition counts.
Principle 3: Measure Contact Quality, Not Contact Quantity
Emphasize connected calls over total calls (contact rate, not dial count), appointments set over contacts
made  (sales  skill),  show  rate  over  appointments  set  (discipline),  and  close  rate  over  total  activity
(outcomes, not effort).
Principle 4: Speed-to-Lead Is Non-Negotiable
Internet lead response time is the single most impactful metric. Track response_time_minutes  on every
activity. Alert within 15 minutes. Escalate at 30 minutes. Show as top-level dashboard metric.
Principle 5: Bayesian Shrinkage Protects New Reps
Store-level priors prevent penalizing new reps with sparse data. A new rep with 3 leads and 1 sale should
not show 33% close rate — the posterior shrinks toward the store baseline (~6–8%). Confidence labels
(low/medium/high) communicate reliability.

Principle 6: Context Flags Prevent Unfair Comparisons
The context module identifies when performance differences are driven by external factors (lead quality,
inventory pressure) vs. rep skill. When lead quality is low, attribute to "external_factor" and recommend
management action.
Principle 7: Separate Recordkeeping from Analytics-Ready Data
The  three-layer  architecture  (raw  events  →  materialized  state  →  aggregated  analytics)  must  be
maintained. Dashboards never query events directly. Analytics should be rebuildable from events.
Principle 8: Design for Coaching, Not Just Tracking
Every metric should answer one of: "What should I do right now?" / "Am I on track?" / "Where am I losing
deals?" / "What should I improve?" / "Who needs help?" — If a feature doesn't answer one of these, it's
recordkeeping.
Principle 9: The Leaderboard Must Be Trustworthy
Rank by won units or revenue — not activity count. Show gross per unit alongside units. Show close rate
alongside  volume.  Use  trust  scores  to  flag  suspicious  patterns.  Never  show  raw  activity  counts  on
leaderboards.
Principle 10: Build for Replay, Not Real-Time Perfection
Event-sourced architecture means TopRep can always rebuild analytics from the event log. This handles
imperfect data quality, evolving requirements, historical analysis, and auditing. Run drift detection weekly
and alert if divergence is found.
